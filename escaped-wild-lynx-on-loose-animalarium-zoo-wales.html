Lynx escaped from The Animalarium zoo in Wales STILL on loose and spotted prowling countryside as zo

 Posted on August 20, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  542 words  |  Aldo Pusey

A DANGEROUS wild lynx is STILL on the prowl after escaping from a zoo in Wales six days ago.

People were warned not to approach Eurasian Lynx Lillith, who has so far evaded farmers, zookeeper patrols, and even two police helicopters.

Lillith escaped from her cage at The Animalarium, a popular tourist attraction in Borth, Cardiganshire, West Wales.

A Dyfed-Powys police spokeswoman said: "We are advising public in the area to be alert and vigilant.

"The animal should not be approached as it could become aggressive if cornered.

"The lynx is unlikely to approach people, but may attempt to take livestock or pets as food.

"It is a mystery how she escaped - we can't find any holes in the cage or overhanging branches she could climb."

Police believe she may stay close to the zoo where her mother Denere is a star attraction.

Another adult female and two of Lillith's siblings are kept in the secure cage at the zoo which also has lions.

But the missing lynx is capable of fending for itself and could wander across country.

Owner Tracy Tweedy, 47, said: "She is not dangerous unless you tried to catch her in which case she is very dangerous and has very sharp teeth and claws.

"We think she will stay nearby and the police helicopter saw her in a field not far away. But she is capable of hunting food for herself so we hope she doesn't go far."

"Lilleth was not captured last night but we did have two confirmed sightings of her on the hill just behind the zoo which means she hasn't gone far.

"We imagine she will go into hiding during the day and will nest down in some bushes close by. Our keepers will be continuing the hunt today and putting out bait traps so we can hopefully catch her and reunite her with her family.

"The park will be open today as normal as Lilleth poses very little threat to the public. For obvious reasons night tours are cancelled until further notice

Baited traps have been set on land close to the Animalarium which was taken over by Mrs Tweedy and her artist husband Dean in May this year.

The couple paid £675,000 for the zoo with 300 animals, including the lynx and two lions called Zulu and Wilma.

The couple's adventure has been likened to the Matt Damon film: "We bought a zoo".

The BBC were filming a documentary at the attraction when Lillith escaped last week.

Mrs Tweedy said: "She is lovely to look at and quite confident. But she is wild, you can't pet her and she has never had human contact."

A plan to reintroduce the lynx into Britain where they lived wild 1,000 years ago was slammed by the National Farmners Union because of the danger to ramblers and livestock.

Police said if anyone sees the lynx attacking another animal, or if they find it trapped or injured they should dial 999.
