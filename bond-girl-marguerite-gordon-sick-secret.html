 Posted on March 25, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  887 words  |  Reinaldo Massengill

ONE of the first Bond girls has revealed how she kept a vicious sexual assault at the hands of a movie director secret for six decades.

In an exclusive interview with The US Sun, Marguerite Gordon alleged that Terence Young, the director of the first Bond movie Dr No, “aggressively” groped and fondled her as they travelled in a limousine to a wrap party for the 007 film.

Speaking on the 60th anniversary of Dr No, the former actress also urged Bond bosses to consider a person of color as the new 007 - but was adamant the role must not go to a woman.

In her first major media interview in decades, Gordon, then known as Marguerite LeWars, claimed that she frantically spurned Young's sexual advances, after which he cut her real voice in the film, any chance of film PR and her invite to the Dr No London premiere.

Defiant Gordon says she had had to slap Young twice to fend off his unwanted advances during the car journey from a hotel to Morgan’s Harbour in Jamaica.

Publicly for six decades, the 82-year-old, who was the first ethnic Bond girl, covered up the assault as “a disagreement” as she didn't want to cause any drama between Jamaican officials who’d invested much money into Dr No.

"I am quite comfortable with staying silent," Gordon said.

"Terence challenged my ethics and morality. I just let it pass and I could not be bothered to speak publicly.

"It was brave of me to stop him assaulting me, and I am not sure anyone had treated him like that before.

"I did not have any desire to get into the movies, my future lay in the airline industry and I was too busy to do anything.

"I know there is a MeToo movement and I was so damn mad at him and upset about that.

'UGLY SITUATION'

"I got annoyed that he changed the voice of the Chung character, but I decided what was the point?

"It was such an ugly situation especially as the man had been so nice before.

"I really couldn't be bothered to speak up. I didn't regard Hollywood as the holy grail."

While, she felt empowered to act against Young, but has sympathy for other actresses, who did succumb to advances from Hollywood tyrants.

“I was twenty two years old and when I read after all these years that you have the Me Too movement, especially in America, where the young girls have been treated very badly that way by directors and people," she said.

“It’s the family strength and therefore, family is very important. And Jamaica was very eager about the film. I have never been invited to any Royal premieres."

Gordon, who is promoting her third novel Empires of The Caribbean , made history as the first female Bond villain - Dr. No's photographer, Annabel Chung.

Today, after being effectively forgotten by Bond executives for 60 years, she said would welcome returning for a walk on part or writing role.

"I would be honored to come back," she said.

"I think it is time for producers to look at a non white Bond."

Marguerite Gordon

Gordon, who lives in Trinidad and Tobago, also told how she hopes stars like Idris Elba and Daniel Kaluuya will be considered for the iconic role, as long "as they are the best fit"

"I think it is time for producers to look at a non white Bond," she said.

"My kind of Jamaican, we don't look at things as black and white. It is about class. We look at the star's screen presence, whether he speaks correctly, is a gentleman and charisma. And he has to be sexy.

"The color doesn't matter to me."

NO TO FEMALE 007

"I have no problem with them being non white, but i just don't want to make it a woman.

"They should stick to writer Ian Fleming's character.

"As a woman I am all about pushing forward women all the time to get better. But I cannot see a woman in the role of what is expected of James Bond. Is she going to jump in bed with a lot of men? I don't like that.

"Bond has to be violent. I would not like to see a woman behave like Bond. There are modes of violence, but I do not know that it would behove a woman."

BEST BOND

She cites Connery as the greatest ever 007, with only Daniel Craig coming close to the true character.

"Sean was extremely important to making Bond.

"He set a high standard. He treated me so well and was debonair, handsome and possessed this dangerous quality and humanness.

"Daniel was good, because he brought the edgy side of Bond back. Like Sean he was very sexy

"I didn't like George Lazenby at all. I have nothing against Australians, but he was not debonair and didn't have the spirit.

"Roger Moore I liked on screen, but was a little bit too soft
